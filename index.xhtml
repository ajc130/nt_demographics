The New Testament of the Christian Bible has been shaping American culture since the Puritans first settled this continent because their interpretation of it did not mesh with the Anglican church’s. American culture has diversified quite a bit since that first colony on Plymouth Rock, but one thing that hasn’t changed is the fact that people who call themselves Christians interpret the New Testament in drastically different ways, and that these different interpretations have far-reaching ramifications regarding how they live their lives, how they think other people ought to live their lives, and how they relate to the government.

In my research, I was particularly interested in the way parts of the New Testament are used as a weapon by Christians on both sides of the American political spectrum, to the extent that they tend to label those with opposite opinions as not actually being Christians. By using digital humanities technologies to gather data on the demographics of the characters in the gospels and the book of Acts as well as the people addresses by Paul in his epistles and comparing that to the interpretations offered by the largest mainline denomination in the US (the United Methodist Church) and largest evangelical denomination (the Southern Baptist Convention), I have attempted to answer questions like:

Who gets Jesus?

Who gets Paul?

Do Jesus and Paul even preach the same gospel?

What would Jesus do in an election year?
